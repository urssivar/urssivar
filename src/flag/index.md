# Kaitag Flag

The flag of the **Kaitag** people (**Хайдаҡӏла**) is a blue field with a white seal at its center.

<FlagHero texture="/assets/images/flag/swallowtail.png" />

The stylized [Kufic monogram](#monogram) from the pantheon of the rulers of Kaitag symbolizes centuries-old statehood.

Above and to the right — a white four-leaf [madder flower](#madder), once abundantly cultivated across Kaitag.

[Deep blue](#blue) — the traditional ground of Kaitag embroidery.

Proportions 2:3 ([swallowtail](#swallowtail) 1:2). Color #0047AB / PMS 300 C. [Spec & files](https://github.com/urssivar/flag).

## Monogram {#monogram}

:::img
![Sarcophagus](/assets/images/flag/sarcophagus.png){.w-full .-translate-y-1/10}
{.h-60 .sm:h-80 .bg-elevated}

The main sarcophagus. Kalakoreish, Dagestan.
:::

The stylized sign traces back to a 13th-century sarcophagus in Kalakoreish.

> On the end face of the sarcophagus, shaped as a pointed arch, a multi-figured relief composition is carved: on either side of a highly stylized tree stand two lions. [...] The top of the tree is crowned by a medallion figure with a pointed tip and a scalloped inner edge. Enclosed within the medallion is a relief sign in the form of a monogram, likewise inspired by Arabic letters.
>
> — M. M. Mammaev, 2014. _Art of Zirikhgeran-Kubachi of the 13th–15th Centuries and Its Place in the System of Artistic Cultures of East and West_

The sarcophagus was most likely erected over the grave of a Kaitag ruler. Its inscription reads _"This is Hizdan."_

> We may suppose that we are looking at representatives of the dynasty of Kaitag rulers.
>
> — M. G. Magomedov, A. R. Shikhsaidov, 2000. _Kalakoreish (Fortress of the Qurayshites)_

## Madder Flower {#madder}

:::img
![Rose Madder](/assets/images/flag/madder.png){.rotate-25 .sm:scale-75 .-translate-y-1/4 .sm:-translate-y-1/3}
{.h-60 .sm:h-80 .bg-elevated}

_Rubia tinctorum._ Franz Eugen Köhler, 1887. _Köhler’s Medizinal-Pflanzen_
:::

Madder (_Rubia tinctorum_) shaped both the economy and the visual culture of Kaitag.

> Madder grew in abundance in Kaitag: many had whole plantations of it, from which they earned more income than from growing wheat. Other dye plants also grew here and were used as colorants for silk threads. All these factors contributed to Kaitag becoming the center of Kaitag embroidery production.
>
> — Z. I. Gasanova, F. A. Gadzhalova, 2022. _Kaitag Embroidery: from its origins to the future_

## Deep Blue {#blue}

:::img
![Three-lobe beggarticks](/assets/images/flag/embroidery.png){.rotate-25 .sm:scale-75 .-translate-y-1/4 .sm:-translate-y-1/3}
{.h-60 .sm:h-80 .bg-elevated}

"Spiraling Horns." R. Chenciner, 1993. _Kaitag: Textile Art from Daghestan_
:::

Dark blue is the traditional ground of Kaitag embroidery, documented by ethnographers in the early 20th century.

> The term "Kaitag embroidery" was introduced into scholarly use by the prominent ethnographer and Caucasus specialist E. M. Shilling. [...] he wrote of the "cottage production" of "cushion embroideries" among the Kaitags: "The cushion embroideries are large rectangular pieces of homespun fabric (of linen, hemp, or cotton), dyed dark blue and embroidered in large satin stitch."
>
> — Z. I. Gasanova, F. A. Gadzhalova, 2022. _Kaitag Embroidery: from its origins to the future_

## Swallowtail {#swallowtail}

:::img
![Banners](/assets/images/flag/banners.png){.rotate-25 .sm:scale-75 .-translate-y-1/4 .sm:-translate-y-1/3}
{.h-60 .sm:h-80 .bg-elevated}

Banners of the mountain warriors. M. Korkmasov, G. Baliev, Z. Arukhov.
:::

The two-tailed form recalls the banner tradition of the Caucasian War.

> No. 3. Banner of Eldar-Bek of Kara-Kaitag. White, two-tailed, without inscription. Captured in battle on August 24, 1831. The battle took place at a Muslim cemetery near Derbent, where the banner flew atop one of the forward tombs.
>
> — Yu. U. Dadaev, 2009. _The Naibs and Mudirs of Shamil_
